# Tyler Cowen on the decay rate of metrics:

Tyler Cowen on the decay rate of metrics:

When we measure GDP, we change what GDP measures. When we measure reading comprehension via standardized tests, schools teach to the test. When we measure AI alignment via human preference data, the model learns to game preference data. The pattern is universal — every measurement that becomes a target stops measuring what we cared about.

The interesting question isn't whether this happens. It's how *fast* it happens, and what makes some metrics more durable than others. My hypothesis: the rate of decay scales with the tightness of the optimization loop. Markets adapt to new tax rules in months. Reward models drift in batches. Test-prep industries take years. The faster the feedback, the faster the metric collapses into proxy.

If that's right, then the design problem isn't 'pick a better metric'. It's 'engineer slack into the loop.' Friction, lag, multiple imperfectly-correlated metrics — these aren't inefficiencies, they're load-bearing.
